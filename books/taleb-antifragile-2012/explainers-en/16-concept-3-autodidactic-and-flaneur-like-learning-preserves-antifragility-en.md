## Core idea

Learning chosen, assembled, and tested by the person himself preserves freedom, curiosity, and asymmetry better than centralized curricula do.

## Detailed explanation

Self-directed learning behaves like a barbell. School is kept at the minimum safe level, while the real upside comes from open reading, drift, and curiosity outside the syllabus.

Taleb wants a positive alternative, not just an attack on schools. The antidote is self-directed exposure. In the whole book, the flaneur theme continues into Taleb's defense of opaque practical knowledge in chapter 17.

## What this example is really showing

Taleb tells the autobiographical story directly. He studied the minimum needed to pass school, then read voraciously on his own, sometimes thirty to sixty hours a week. Later, when probability obsessed him, he ordered almost every book with `probability` or `stochastic` in the title and taught himself by following curiosity rather than a curriculum.
