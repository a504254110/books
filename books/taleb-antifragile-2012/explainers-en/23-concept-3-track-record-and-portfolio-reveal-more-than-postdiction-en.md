## Core idea

In uncertain domains the cleanest way to judge belief is to inspect what a person actually risked and what actually happened, not the story he tells later. 

## Detailed explanation

This matters because postdiction has a free option built into it. A speaker can forget wrong forecasts, keep the few statements that fit the present, and look wise after the event. 

This gives the reader a practical audit tool for experts, economists, journalists, and doctors. It also connects back to Thales, Fat Tony, and Taleb's preference for payoff over rhetorical correctness. 

A common confusion is this: Portfolio alignment does not prove that the person is right. It proves only that he is exposed enough for his opinion to count as more than cheap talk. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb's flagship case is Joseph Stiglitz and the Fannie Mae report. Stiglitz helped bless the system before it blew up, then later wrote as if he had predicted the crisis. Taleb's rule follows directly: do not ask for the opinion first; ask what is in the person's portfolio.
