## Core idea

For nonperishable things, additional survival can increase expected future life instead of shortening it. 

## Detailed explanation

This matters because it explains why age can be evidence in books, ideas, and technologies. Survival itself filters out hidden weakness. 

The Lindy effect becomes Taleb's reading rule, technology rule, and cultural rule. It also supports his later distrust of recently invented diseases, solutions, and buzzwords. 

A common confusion is this: Lindy is a probabilistic heuristic, not a guarantee about every single old technology or institution. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb contrasts people with nonperishable things. A forty-year-old person has less life ahead than he did last year. But a forty-year-old book that is still in print can be expected to last longer than a ten-year-old book. Richard Gott's comparison between the Great Pyramid and the Berlin Wall makes the same point in a vivid way.
