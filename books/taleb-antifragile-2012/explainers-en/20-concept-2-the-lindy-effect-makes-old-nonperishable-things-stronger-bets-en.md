# Chapter 20 · Concept 2 · English explainer

**Back to concept:** [[20-time-and-fragility#Concept 2: The Lindy effect makes old nonperishable things stronger bets|Back to chapter 20 · Concept 2]]

## Core idea

For nonperishable things, additional survival can increase expected future life instead of shortening it.

## Detailed explanation

Age can be evidence in books, ideas, and technologies. Survival itself filters out hidden weakness.

Lindy is a probabilistic heuristic, not a guarantee about every single old technology or institution.

## What this example is really showing

Taleb contrasts people with nonperishable things. A forty-year-old person has less life ahead than he did last year. But a forty-year-old book that is still in print can be expected to last longer than a ten-year-old book. He then cites the physicist Richard Gott, who uses old monuments to make the same point: something like the Great Pyramid has already survived so long that it is a stronger longevity bet than a much younger structure such as the Berlin Wall.

The point of the scene: Age can be evidence in books, ideas, and technologies. Survival itself filters out hidden weakness.
