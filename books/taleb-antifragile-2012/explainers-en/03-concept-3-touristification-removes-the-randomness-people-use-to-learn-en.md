## Core idea

Modern systems increasingly script experience, remove uncertainty, and treat people as if they had simple mechanical inputs and outputs. That process weakens the very systems it intends to protect. 

## Detailed explanation

These scenes show why Taleb attacks touristification. The friction is not valuable because it is annoying; it is valuable because it forces attention, improvisation, and skill-building that a fully managed script removes. 

This is the social and civilizational version of the organism-machine contrast. Taleb is showing how modernity misclassifies humans. In the whole book, the argument leads into the next chapters on modernity, intervention, and the ways centralized management suppresses useful disorder. 

A common confusion is this: Taleb is not saying every inconvenience is good. His target is a world that removes all unpredictability and, with it, the need to adapt. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb gives two linked travel scenes. Chad improves his Russian after being trapped in a Moscow hospital quarantine and forced to deal with confusion, pressure, and Tolstoy in the original. Taleb then contrasts that with modern travel assistants who remove ambiguity, solve everything in English, and leave the traveler with comfort but less exposure and less learning.
