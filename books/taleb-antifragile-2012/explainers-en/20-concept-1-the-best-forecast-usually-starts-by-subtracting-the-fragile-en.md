## Core idea

In complex systems, the most reliable prediction method is often to ask what will break, not to imagine what new thing will appear.

## Detailed explanation

It flips the normal future-talk instinct. Durable things often stay with us, while many fragile fashionable additions quietly disappear.

This is `via negativa` applied to forecasting. It also links chapter 20 back to chapter 19: fragility detection is useful because the future is easier to read through likely failure than through detailed positive visions.

## What this example is really showing

Taleb mocks old future fantasies by describing his own ordinary dinner. He still expects taverns, chairs, wine, silverware, cheese, shoes, and taxis, not flying motorcycles and nutritionally optimized pills. In the same spirit, when `The Economist` asked him about 2036, he predicted bookshelves, telephones, artisans, and the weakening of fragile large systems.

The point of the scene: It flips the normal future-talk instinct. Durable things often stay with us, while many fragile fashionable additions quietly disappear.
