## Core idea

Interventionists overreact because they respond to fluctuations that are not informative. They treat random variation as meaningful signal and feel licensed to step in. 

## Detailed explanation

These scenes explain why modern interventionists make so many bad calls. The more often they sample noisy data, the more they mistake background variation for meaningful information and act when they should ignore it. 

This explains why modern institutions intervene too often even when they have good intentions. The informational filter is broken. In the whole book, the next chapter turns from intervention to prediction, showing that false signal extraction also corrupts forecasting. 

A common confusion is this: Taleb is not asking people to ignore strong signals. He is arguing that professionals often react to tiny fluctuations that do not deserve action at all. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb compares the neurotic person who turns every pimple into cancer with the calm person who only reacts to real danger. He then gives modern versions: a personal doctor who keeps intervening to justify his role, Greenspan staring at vacuum-cleaner sales in Cleveland, and newspapers filling pages with anecdotal noise every day.
