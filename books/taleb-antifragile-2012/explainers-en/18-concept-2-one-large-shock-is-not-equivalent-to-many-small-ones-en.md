## Core idea

In nonlinear systems, equal averages hide unequal outcomes. The path and distribution of shocks matter more than the arithmetic mean. 

## Detailed explanation

This matters because the average alone does not tell you what the body, system, or market experienced. Distribution changes the outcome. 

Taleb needs this point to explain why smoothing arguments and average-based policy thinking repeatedly fail. In the whole book, jensen's inequality in chapter 19 formalizes this same intuition more explicitly. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb uses two health examples to make the point vivid. One brother walks a mile steadily, while the other waits and then sprints the same distance in the same total time. The second can get more training benefit. He also contrasts a balanced daily intake with irregular fasting and refeeding, arguing that variability itself changes the biological response. 

What the reader should notice in this scene is this: This matters because the average alone does not tell you what the body, system, or market experienced. Distribution changes the outcome.
