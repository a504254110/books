## Core idea

In optional domains, drift, skewed payoffs, and repeated reinvention are normal. People who look only at averages or fixed plans will misread the process. 

## Detailed explanation

This matters because optional progress does not look tidy while it is happening. Plans change, most attempts look mediocre, and the upside is concentrated in a few outcomes that are easy to miss if you judge too early. 

This is where Taleb links rewritten history to his statistical view of optionality. Later chapters formalize the same insight under convexity and inverse-turkey logic: good news can stay hidden in past samples. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb uses drifting companies to make the point: Coca-Cola began as a pharmaceutical product, Tiffany as a stationery store, Nokia as a paper mill, and Avon in door-to-door book sales. He then warns that in optional businesses like biotech, many firms can show no profit while a few giant winners drive the total result.
