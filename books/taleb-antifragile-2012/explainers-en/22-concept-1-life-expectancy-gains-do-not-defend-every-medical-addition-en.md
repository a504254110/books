## Core idea

A rise in life expectancy does not prove that every modern intervention is good, because the gains came from a mixed bundle of causes and a few very large wins. 

## Detailed explanation

This matters because it blocks a lazy inference. A broad historical improvement is not a blank check for any new intervention sold under the same banner. 

This keeps chapter 21's medical asymmetry rule from being buried under demographic rhetoric. Taleb is still asking which interventions have large visible upside and which ones smuggle in hidden downside. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb attacks the common argument `we live longer now, so modern intervention must be good`. He answers that the big gains came from things like sanitation, emergency care, and major surgery, not from every later pill, metric, or comfort-focused addition.
