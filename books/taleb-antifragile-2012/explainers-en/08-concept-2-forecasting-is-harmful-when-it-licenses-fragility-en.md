# Chapter 8 · Concept 2 · English explainer

## Core idea

The real cost of prediction is not embarrassment after error; it is the false confidence that encourages people to build systems with no margin for surprise. 

## Detailed explanation

This matters because prediction is not neutral. A bad forecast can actively increase fragility by making people take extra risk, invest with less margin, or trust a system that should have been redesigned. 

Taleb wants the reader to see prediction as iatrogenic. It can make exposure worse even when nobody notices immediately. In the whole book, this argument reappears in the ethical chapters, where Taleb asks whether forecasters pay for the consequences of their advice. 

A common confusion is this: Taleb is not saying wrong forecasts are the main problem because they embarrass experts. His point is that forecasts become harmful when institutions act on them. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb cites evidence, shown to him by Kahneman, that even random numerical forecasts increase risk taking. He compares this to snake-oil medicine: the false remedy is dangerous not because it is merely wrong, but because it changes behavior in the wrong direction.
