## Core idea

More is not just more. In many systems, doubling exposure more than doubles harm, which means size itself creates fragility.

## Detailed explanation

Size creates squeezes. Large units lose options faster, and when stress arrives they must act at the worst possible moment and price.

This explains why centralization, concentration, and full-capacity operation often look efficient right until they fail. In the whole book, this supports the later arguments on political size, financial concentration, and medical dose-response.

## What this example is really showing

Taleb uses three squeeze scenes. A person can keep a cat when resources tighten, but an elephant becomes a disaster. A giant bank hit by one rogue trader, Jérôme Kerviel at Société Générale, must unwind tens of billions in a hurry and crashes the price against itself. He makes the same point with crowded theaters and stadiums: if too many people depend on the same narrow exit, one disturbance becomes a bottleneck.

The point of the scene: Size creates squeezes. Large units lose options faster, and when stress arrives they must act at the worst possible moment and price.
