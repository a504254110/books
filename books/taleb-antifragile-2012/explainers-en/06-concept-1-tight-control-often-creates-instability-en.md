## Core idea

Systems can become less stable when they are regulated too closely. Fine-tuned correction tends to overshoot and amplify disturbances.

## Detailed explanation

These scenes show the same pattern in two settings. When small variation is suppressed too hard, the system stops adjusting naturally and becomes more fragile to the next disturbance.

Taleb is turning an intuition about politics and organizations into a general systems claim. More intervention is not automatically more control. In the whole book, this logic leads straight into chapter 7, where Taleb names the human version of the same mistake: naive intervention.

## What this example is really showing

Taleb starts with Maxwell's steam-engine governors. A light correction can stabilize the engine, but overly close compensation makes the machine oscillate and even break apart. He then adds the human version: a person who arrives home at exactly six every day creates anxiety the first time he is a few minutes late, while a person with a slightly variable schedule does not.

The point of the scene: These scenes show the same pattern in two settings. When small variation is suppressed too hard, the system stops adjusting naturally and becomes more fragile to the next disturbance.
