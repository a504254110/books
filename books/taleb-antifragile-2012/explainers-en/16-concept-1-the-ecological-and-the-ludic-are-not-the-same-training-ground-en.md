## Core idea

Skills developed in explicit-rule games often fail to transfer into real life, where rules are partial, hidden, and changing.

## Detailed explanation

A closed-rule environment trains one kind of skill only. The moment the rules stop being explicit, success in the game no longer guarantees success in life.

Taleb wants to block a common modern inference: success in controlled educational environments does not automatically translate into ecological competence. In the whole book, this supports chapter 17's defense of practical heuristics that cannot be fully justified in classroom language.

## What this example is really showing

Taleb contrasts blackjack and chess with real life. In games the rules are known in advance. In life they are not. He adds a classroom example: children who have not learned arithmetic correctly count fourteen intervals between fifteen poles, while children just taught arithmetic often get confused and answer fifteen.
