## Core idea

Taleb says ordinary language is missing an important category. A thing can be fragile, robust, or antifragile. 

## Detailed explanation

The example separates `robust` from `antifragile`. A robust package can survive rough handling. An antifragile package benefits from rough handling. 

This is the base distinction for the whole book. If a reader treats `antifragile` as just another word for `resilient`, later chapters on optionality, medicine, and ethics become much flatter. 

A common confusion is this: A common mistake is to think Taleb is only renaming resilience. His actual point is that resilience returns to baseline, while antifragility improves through stress. 

## What this example is really showing

A normal package marked `fragile` tells people to handle it carefully because shaking or dropping it will damage what is inside. Taleb then asks the reader to imagine the real opposite kind of package: one that improves when it is shaken or dropped. 

What the reader should notice in this scene is this: The example separates `robust` from `antifragile`. A robust package can survive rough handling. An antifragile package benefits from rough handling.
