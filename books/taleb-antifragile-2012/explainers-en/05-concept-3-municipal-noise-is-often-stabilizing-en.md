# Chapter 5 · Concept 3 · English explainer

**Back to concept:** [[05-the-souk-and-the-office-building#Concept 3: Municipal noise is often stabilizing|Back to chapter 5 · Concept 3]]

## Core idea

Taleb argues that local noise can protect a political system. Many small frictions at the bottom may be safer than one smooth center that can spread one large mistake across the whole country.

## Detailed explanation

The important word here is `noise`. Taleb does not mean ideal harmony or elegant governance. He means small local fights, bargaining, variation, inconsistency, and adjustment.

His claim is structural. When power is distributed across smaller units, mistakes are more likely to stay local. One canton, municipality, or region may make a bad choice, but the damage is less likely to become national at once. That kind of messiness looks inefficient, yet it can protect the larger system from one synchronized disaster.

Centralized order has the opposite temptation. It looks clean because one center can suppress variation, impose one plan, and reduce local friction. Taleb thinks that visual calm is often dangerous. A single ideology, policy, or administrative error can then scale across the whole population because there are too few local barriers.

So this concept is not praising chaos for its own sake. It is saying that small-scale disorder can act as a safety mechanism when it prevents one totalizing error from dominating the whole structure.

## What this example is really showing

Switzerland is Taleb's main scene for this idea. He presents it as a confederation with cantons, municipalities, local peculiarities, and many petty disputes. That picture is not meant to sound glamorous. It is meant to show a system where variation remains distributed.

The contrast is the modern centralized state or large office-building mindset, where one center wants uniformity and control. That kind of system may look calmer because fewer disagreements are visible, but the risk is that one big error can travel much farther.

So the example is really showing why `local disorder` and `systemic fragility` are not opposites. Some local disorder prevents systemic fragility by keeping shocks, conflicts, and bad decisions from becoming fully synchronized.
