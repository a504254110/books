## Core idea

It is a mistake to assume that because prosperous places often contain prestigious educational institutions, importing the institutions will reproduce the prosperity.

## Detailed explanation

It separates two different arrows. Wealth can buy education and credentials, but that does not prove education created the wealth in the first place.

Taleb is attacking a favorite modern shortcut: treating symbolic markers of success as if they were its engine. In the whole book, the next chapter expands this skepticism into a wholesale rewrite of the standard story about technology and science.

## What this example is really showing

Taleb opens the chapter in Abu Dhabi, watching oil wealth import entire foreign universities as if prestige campuses could be poured into the desert and turned into organic knowledge. He then uses country-level contrasts such as Taiwan versus the Philippines and Korea versus Argentina to show that rising education levels do not map neatly onto rising national wealth.

The point of the scene: It separates two different arrows. Wealth can buy education and credentials, but that does not prove education created the wealth in the first place.
