## Core idea

Slack, buffers, and unused capacity are not waste when systems are concave to overload. They are protection against disproportionate harm. 

## Detailed explanation

This matters because what looks like efficiency on an average day becomes chaos under stress. Redundancy is not cosmetic waste in such systems; it is what prevents concavity from turning a small disturbance into a cascade. 

Taleb is attacking the modern managerial instinct to run everything close to full utilization. In the whole book, the medical chapters later rest heavily on the same nonlinear reasoning about dose and overload. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb gives traffic and transport as the clearest picture. A 10 percent rise in cars can increase travel time by 50 percent, and one partial bridge closure can turn minutes of delay into hours. He extends the same logic to Heathrow, to airports run near full capacity, and to projects and wars that almost never finish early but often explode in time and cost.
