## Core idea

Institutions without expiration, turnover, or occasional disorder become fragile because they can no longer renew themselves from within.

## Detailed explanation

The point is not that random leadership is ideal in every setting. The point is that a system needs some way to reshuffle power and remove stale incumbents before rigidity turns into fragility.

Taleb is extending antifragility from physical systems to elite circulation and political order. In the whole book, this concern with blocked renewal will resurface in the ethics chapters, where Taleb attacks institutions that suppress downside for insiders.

## What this example is really showing

Taleb imagines randomizing rulers instead of randomizing citizens, and notes that Athens used lotteries to pick members of the assembly. He pairs that with modern examples of bosses, board members, politicians, and tenured figures who stay too long and block renewal.
