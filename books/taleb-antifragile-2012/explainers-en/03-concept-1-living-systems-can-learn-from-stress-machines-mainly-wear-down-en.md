## Core idea

Within a range, living systems can use stress to adapt, while machines usually only accumulate wear or break. 

## Detailed explanation

These scenes show the key contrast. In a living system, some pressure can trigger repair and strengthening. In a machine, repeated pressure usually means damage. 

Taleb needs a hard distinction between the organic and the engineered. Without it, people keep importing engineering intuitions into domains governed by living adaptation. In the whole book, this distinction underlies the later attacks on overengineering in politics, education, and medicine. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb gives several body-level scenes. Bones become denser under repeated weight-bearing stress, but become weaker in zero gravity or when they are not carrying enough load. Skin on the hands thickens after manual work. A washing machine under repeated jolts does not adapt in any of these ways; it simply wears down. 

What the reader should notice in this scene is this: These scenes show the key contrast. In a living system, some pressure can trigger repair and strengthening. In a machine, repeated pressure usually means damage.
