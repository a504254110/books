## Core idea

In many domains, especially medicine and policy, people intervene because doing something feels responsible, not because the expected payoff has been properly compared with the possible harm.

## Detailed explanation

This scene shows why Taleb insists on a break-even question. When the decision rule is this unstable, the intervention may be driven more by the urge to act than by a clear net benefit.

This is Taleb's main diagnosis of modern overmanagement. The problem is not only wrong action but the unexamined compulsion to act. In the whole book, this becomes the basis for the medical asymmetry rules in chapters 21 and 22.

## What this example is really showing

Taleb opens with a 1930s tonsillectomy study. One group of doctors recommended surgery for 174 children. A second group then recommended it for 99 of the remaining children, and a third group still recommended it for 52 more. The same children kept moving from `needs surgery` to `does not need surgery` depending on which doctor saw them.
