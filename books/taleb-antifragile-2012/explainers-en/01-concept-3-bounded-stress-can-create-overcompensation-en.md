## Core idea

Taleb's first mechanism for antifragility is overcompensation. A limited stress can make a system build extra strength. 

## Detailed explanation

These scenes move the chapter beyond myth. They show that the same harmful force can have different effects at different doses, and that a limited hit can trigger extra strength instead of just repair. 

This is the bridge from vocabulary to mechanism. It leads directly into chapter 2 and later supports Taleb's criticism of overprotection and excessive intervention. 

A common confusion is this: The main question is not whether hormesis exists in biology. The harder question is how far Taleb can extend this pattern into politics, economics, and institutions. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb starts with Mithridates, who took small doses of poison until he could tolerate larger ones. He then moves to `hormesis`: Hugo Schulz observed that small doses of poison stimulated yeast growth, while larger doses caused harm. He adds the broader pattern of caloric restriction, where limited hunger may trigger healthier responses than constant abundance.
