## Core idea

In real decisions, people usually act on consequences and exposure, not on pure truth conditions or probability statements. 

## Detailed explanation

This matters because it changes the decision frame. The relevant question is often not `Is it true?` but `What happens to me if this low-probability event is real?` 

This is the chapter's decisive bridge into Book V. Once payoff outranks abstract truth, Taleb can formalize fragility as an exposure shape rather than as a belief problem. 

## What this example is really showing

Taleb gives ordinary cases: you still screen airline passengers even though the probability that any one traveler is a terrorist is tiny, you still spend heavily on nuclear safety even if explosion is unlikely, and you often refuse a random medicine even without a formal proof that it will harm you.
