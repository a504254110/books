## Core idea

Taleb says Thales mattered because he built an asymmetric deal, not because he had a perfect theory about the future.

## Detailed explanation

Taleb wants to move the reader's admiration away from prediction and toward payoff design. A theory about the harvest could be incomplete, fragile, or impossible to defend. Thales still wins if the structure of the deal is right.

That is why the key contrast is not `good theory vs bad theory`. It is `ordinary risky bet vs asymmetrical option`. Thales did not need precision. He needed a position where being wrong was survivable and being right had large upside.

## What this example is really showing

Thales of Miletus was a poor philosopher who was mocked for not making money. While it was still winter, before the olive harvest, he paid small deposits to reserve the seasonal use of olive presses around Miletus and Chios. If the harvest stayed weak, he would mainly lose those small deposits. But if the harvest turned out strong, growers would suddenly need the presses, and he would control something valuable.

So the story is not that Thales saw the future more perfectly than everyone else. It is that he arranged the asymmetry before the uncertainty was resolved.
