## Chapter in one minute

> [!abstract]
> - The title means the people with time, prestige, and protected positions often get to write the history, even when they were not the ones who built the thing.
> - Taleb argues that theory is often written after practice, then presented as if theory had led practice all along.
> - The chapter uses finance, engineering, medicine, and business history to show how optionality and tinkering keep getting erased by cleaner stories.

> [!info]- Grounding

## What you should be able to say out loud

- Official histories often retrofit theory onto success and erase luck, drift, and failed attempts.
- Practice frequently comes before theory, with explanation arriving later and claiming credit.
- In optional domains, neat plans and average-looking data can hide where the real progress came from.

## Key distinctions

- Retrospective explanation vs actual process
- Practice-first vs theory-first
- Adaptive entrepreneurship vs plan execution
- Selection story vs authored story

## Common confusions in this chapter

- Taleb is not saying all theory is fake. He is saying much of the prestige story about where useful knowledge came from is backward.
- He is also not saying every academic acts in bad faith. The stronger claim is that institutions systematically reward clean narratives over messy formation.

## Core concepts

### Concept 1: Official histories retrofit theory onto practical success

> [!concept]
> **Claim.** Many historical accounts are written from the perspective of later interpreters who attribute success to the kinds of knowledge they personally value.
>
> **Example.** Taleb starts with derivatives traders using complex option payoffs successfully before the academic formulas were derived. He and derivatives practitioner and historian Espen Haug then trace how academics later packaged those formulas as if traders had been using theory first. Taleb even describes an editor trying to rewrite his firsthand account so the academic arrow would point the preferred way.
>
> **Why this example matters.** This matters because it shows the mechanism of expropriation directly. Once practice works, the cleaner written account often overwrites the actual order of events.
>
> **Whole-book connection.** Taleb is exposing the narrative laundering that protects academic prestige while downgrading practitioners. In the whole book, this prepares the later ethical critique of professions that profit from distortions without paying for them.
>

### Concept 2: Engineering and architecture often worked before theory caught up

> [!concept]
> **Claim.** Theoretical articulation frequently follows practical problem solving rather than causing it. What works becomes legible after the fact.
>
> **Example.** Taleb stacks examples from several fields: jet engines were built through trial and adjustment before theory could fully explain them, cybernetics was in practice before Wiener was credited with founding it, and medieval builders raised cathedrals and Roman engineers built aqueducts with heuristics long before formal mathematics became central.
>
> **Why this example matters.** This matters because it breaks the default school story. In many domains, practice is not the last step of theory. It is the source from which later theory is abstracted.
>
> **Whole-book connection.** This is Taleb's most direct challenge to the linear model that runs from science to technology to wealth. In the whole book, this reinforces the optionality and tinkering logic from chapters 12 and 13.
>
> **Deep dive.** [[15-concept-2-engineering-and-architecture-often-worked-before-theory-caught-up-en|English]] · [[15-concept-2-engineering-and-architecture-often-worked-before-theory-caught-up-zh|中文]]
>
> **Grounding.** PDF pp. 238-243; chapter map, PDF p. 13.

### Concept 3: Medicine advanced through empirics and serendipity more than design narratives admit

> [!concept]
> **Claim.** Medical progress is often retold as science-led design, but many of the major gains came from blind chemistry, side effects, and practical uptake of accidental findings.
>
> **Example.** Taleb points to chemotherapy emerging from mustard-gas effects, drugs finding major uses other than the ones they were designed for, and the postwar therapeutic revolution coming from synthetic chemistry that blindly delivered remedies before anyone had a neat story for them.
>
> **Why this example matters.** This matters because medicine is one of the places where people most want to believe in top-down design. Taleb uses it to show that even high-stakes progress often came from messy discovery, adoption, and drift.
>
> **Whole-book connection.** The medical case is important because Taleb will later attack iatrogenics, interventionism, and overconfidence in formal experts. This chapter gives the historical background for those later attacks.
>

### Concept 4: Optional progress looks messy to planners and statisticians

> [!concept]
> **Claim.** In optional domains, drift, skewed payoffs, and repeated reinvention are normal. People who look only at averages or fixed plans will misread the process.
>
> **Example.** Taleb uses drifting companies to make the point: Coca-Cola began as a pharmaceutical product, Tiffany as a stationery store, Nokia as a paper mill, and Avon in door-to-door book sales. He then warns that in optional businesses like biotech, many firms can show no profit while a few giant winners drive the total result.
>
> **Why this example matters.** This matters because optional progress does not look tidy while it is happening. Plans change, most attempts look mediocre, and the upside is concentrated in a few outcomes that are easy to miss if you judge too early.
>
> **Whole-book connection.** This is where Taleb links rewritten history to his statistical view of optionality. Later chapters formalize the same insight under convexity and inverse-turkey logic: good news can stay hidden in past samples.
>

## Points to debate

- Does Taleb overcorrect against theory by treating after-the-fact explanation as mostly distortion?
- How much of history's bias is unavoidable simplification rather than ideological capture?
- Can narrative ever be repaired enough to become genuinely useful in uncertain domains?

## Why this chapter matters in the whole book

- Chapter 14 showed that relevant knowledge is often opaque; this chapter shows how history later buries that fact.
- The attack on linear causation reinforces Taleb's broader case for optionality and trial and error.
- The entrepreneurial examples connect back to chapter 4's salute to risk takers.
- The emphasis on adaptive people carries forward into the flaneur and education chapters.
